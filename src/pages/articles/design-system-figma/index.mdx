A good design system doesn't just help scale products—it enables frictionless collaboration between design and development. In this article, we explore how to build a solid design system in Figma, what to include, what to avoid, and how to keep it flexible.

---

## 🎯 What is a Design System?

A design system is a **collection of principles, visual patterns, and reusable components** that ensure consistency across digital products.

> A good system improves team communication, reduces tech debt, speeds up design cycles, and ensures visual and experiential consistency.

---

## 🧱 What Should a Good Design System Include?

At minimum, a solid system should include:

- **Foundations**: typography, color palette, spacing, grid, and tokens.
- **UI Components**: buttons, inputs, cards, badges, modals, tabs, etc.
- **Layout and Patterns**: headers, sidebars, forms, etc.
- **Documentation**: how to use, when to use, variants, and edge cases.
- **Clear Naming**: `Button / Primary`, `Input / Disabled`, etc.
- **Shared Tokens** (ideally managed with tools like Style Dictionary or Tokens Studio)

📌 Example structure in Figma:

```
Design System /
  ├── Foundations
  │     ├── Colors
  │     ├── Typography
  │     └── Spacing
  ├── Components
  │     ├── Buttons
  │     ├── Forms
  │     └── Cards
  └── Guidelines
        ├── Accessibility
        ├── Layouts
        └── Usage Examples
```

---

## ❌ What to Avoid When Building a System

Avoid these common pitfalls:

- **Over-specifying**: Too much rigidity stifles creativity.
- **Contextless Components**: Without real examples, adoption is hard.
- **Lack of Maintenance**: A system that doesn’t evolve becomes irrelevant.
- **Poor Documentation**: If it's not documented, it won't be used.
- **Inconsistencies**: Watch for visual drift across themes and breakpoints.

---

## 🛠 Practical Tips

- Use **atomic naming** and treat tokens as a **single source of truth**.
- Audit the system regularly and remove unused elements.
- Keep **feedback loops open** with both devs and designers.
- Document real-world examples (inputs with validation, buttons with loading, etc).
- Bake in **accessibility** from the beginning.
